                                                                        Andrea Corbellini marcusklaas 2 years ago

                                                                        If you seek a mathematical proof, then use the algebraic method to solve the equations P + Q = -Q, P + Q = -P and P + Q = 0 (these are the three equations that formalize the sentence "there is no third point R"). You'll see that in the first two cases, the line PQ will be tangent to the curve, while in the third case you'll have P = -Q (that is, a vertical line).

                                                                        If instead you seek an intuitive justification, then try to play with y<sup>2</sup> = x<sup>3</sup> + 1 (which is "one piece"). Try to find two points P and Q such that there is no distinct point R. After trying for a while, you'll notice that the curve always "intercepts" the line somehow, thanks to its convexity (or concavity, depending where you look from).

                                                                        I don't know how to better explain this fact in words, what can I say is that convexity and concavity are the two keywords that should help you.

                                                                                            Andrea Corbellini Michael 2 years ago

                                                                                            Hi Michael. Excellent question! I'll answer you with an example, rather than a general proof. I hope you don't mind.

                                                                                            As you can see in one of the first images of the post, one curve with a singularity is y<sup>2</sup> = x<sup>3</sup>. This curve has a "cusp" (also known as "triple point") in P = (0, 0).

                                                                                            If you try to perform any point addition involving P (either by using the equations, or by using the interactive tool), you'll immediately notice that P + Q = P, for any point Q. If we were in a group, every point would have an inverse and we could write P + Q + (-P) = P + (-P), hence Q = 0. But we have seen that the equation P + Q = P is valid for every Q, not just for Q = 0, hence the group properties don't hold, hence we can't be in a group.

                                                                                            Note that this doesn't necessarily mean that we can't invent a new group law for singular curves. It just means that our group law doesn't work with singular curves.

                                                                                              Jan-Pieter Cornet 2 years ago

                                                                                              Thanks for this article! Especially the (interactive) graphs make things very clear.

                                                                                              Another article that helped me understand how elliptic curve math/crypto worked, was this: http://blog.cr.yp.to/201403...
                                                                                              However, that has an angle towards (implementation) security, and describes Edwards curves as an alternative to the Weierstrass curves that you use. Might still be an interesting read for some of your audience, though!

                                                                                              One of the problem with secure implementations of encryption algorithms is that you need to make sure to solve every equation in constant time, independent of the inputs. Otherwise, you might leak secret information through a timing-attack. Since the Weierstrass curves have exceptions for P=Q or P=0, that makes calculating in constant time really hard.
