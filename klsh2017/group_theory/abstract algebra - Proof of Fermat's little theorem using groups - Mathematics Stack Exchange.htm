    Proof of Fermat's little theorem using groups

    up vote 5 down vote favorite
    2

    I am trying to prove Fermat's little theorem using groups. Here is my proof:

    Let pp be a prime. Since all numbers a such that 1≤a≤p−11≤a≤p−1 are relatively prime with pp, they form a group under multiplication modulo pp.(There is a theorem about this, that must be true, they should be forming a group). So, let the group be G={a,a2,…,ap−1}G={a,a2,…,ap−1} and binary operation is multiplication modulo pp. Since all elements of this group are relatively prime with pp, then any element a∈Ga∈G is a generator of this group, and this group is cyclic. So, we must have a⋅ap−1=amodpa⋅ap−1=amodp. Then multiplying each sides with a−1a−1 (which exists since this is a group) we get ap−1=1modpap−1=1modp.

    Is there any mistake in my proof? I appreciate your helps. Thank you

    As pointed out by Thomas Andrews, one cannot assume that aa generates the group (extreme example: a=1a=1).

    However, aa does generate a subgroup, and the order of that subgroup divides p−1p−1. That will yield a proof.

    There is an elementary proof, that works for abelian groups, and does not require Lagrange or any facts about group orders.

    Let GG be a finite abelian group of order nn. Let a∈Ga∈G. The map x↦axx↦ax is a permutation of GG. Thus

    ∏x∈Gx=∏x∈Gax=an⋅∏x∈Gx,∏x∈Gx=∏x∈Gax=an⋅∏x∈Gx,
    where we have used the fact that GG is abelian.

    It follows an=1an=1.
